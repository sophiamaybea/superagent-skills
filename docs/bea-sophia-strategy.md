## The Business, Fully Defined
**Service:** Hand-drawn illustrated interactive websites built from original artwork, plus adjacent services including therapist/psychologist websites, healthcare professional websites, hospitality and experience business websites, and multilingual website builds.

**Founder assets:** UK-based, multilingual, background in psychology (mother is a psychologist — one live case study site exists), hand-drawn illustration practice, technical web-build capability.

**Market positioning:** Anti-AI, human-made, bespoke. The business exists in direct contrast to the vibe-coding side hustle economy: people who scrape a business's Google listing, paste it into a generative tool, and deliver a site in 20 minutes for several hundred dollars — something that has your name on it, but not your business in it.

**Current state:** No past client portfolio beyond the psychologist site. In a slow revenue period. Needs immediate income alongside long-term brand building.

## Part 1: Hidden Assumptions & Key Unknowns
### 1. The multilingual advantage is massively underused
Speaking multiple languages is not a standard web designer skill — it is a genuine competitive moat. Therapists in France, Spain, Portugal, Germany, and Latin America have the same website problem as UK therapists, but far fewer specialist web designers who speak their language and understand their market. The French-speaking therapist market alone (France, Belgium, Switzerland, Canada's Québec) is enormous. The Spanish-speaking private practice market across Spain and Latin America is even larger. These markets have not yet been saturated by vibe-coders in the same way the English-speaking market has, because the vibe-coders are mostly English-language operators. This is a first-mover advantage that will not last forever — but it exists right now.

### 2. The "anti-AI" positioning is arriving at exactly the right moment
Adobe's 2024 Creative Trends Report recorded a 30% rise in searches for hand-drawn and imperfect design elements. In 2026, hand-drawn illustration and animation has become a primary way for businesses to signal that they care and that their brand is human-first. The "Tactile Rebellion" — a design movement explicitly rejecting AI-generated aesthetics — is naming exactly what this business does as its normal practice. This is not a niche. It is an emerging mainstream preference, and the positioning should be named and claimed explicitly in all marketing.

### 3. Over 75% of web designers say AI has made their job harder — but the ones thriving are the specialists
A 2026 web designer survey found that over 75% say competition from AI has made finding clients more difficult. However, the same survey found that 37% of US web designers earn over $100,000 per year and 78.6% feel properly compensated — suggesting the market has bifurcated. Generalist designers are being squeezed. Specialists with a legible craft and a clear niche are thriving. The illustrated + multilingual + psychologist-specialist positioning puts this business firmly in the thriving category — but only if it is communicated as a specialism, not as general web design.

### 4. The psychologist/therapist niche is the single most validated immediate revenue source
The UK private practice therapy market has thousands of practitioners who need websites. The cost of a professional therapist website ranges from £120 for DIY to over £3,000 for bespoke work. Critically, therapists are actively researching and hiring web designers — it is a mature purchasing behaviour, not a market that needs to be educated. The founder's psychology background means she understands the language, the ethics (GDPR, safeguarding, therapeutic boundaries in copy), and the emotional register of a therapy website in a way a generic designer cannot. This is not a minor differentiator — it is the reason therapists will choose her over a cheaper option.

### 5. The campsite/glamping market is seasonal and increasingly consolidated
The UK caravan and camping market is worth £4.7bn and contains 2,757 registered businesses. However, revenue has been under pressure and larger operators are increasingly working with specialist hospitality web agencies who bundle site + booking software + channel management. The independent glamping and shepherd's hut segment — where bundled agency solutions are less relevant — is the correct target, not larger holiday parks. Timing matters: June to August is when these operators are at peak operational load and thinking about the following year's site.

### 6. The US market for private practice therapy websites is large, underserved by illustrated design, and dollar-priced
Australian, US, and Canadian therapists in private practice all use the same DIY platforms (Squarespace, Wix, Psychology Today profiles) and have the same pain point: generic-looking sites. The US private practice therapy market is significantly larger than the UK equivalent in absolute terms. Charging in USD removes purchasing friction and effectively increases revenue at current exchange rates. An English-language portfolio of one psychologist site plus one or two concept builds is sufficient to reach this market via Instagram, Psychology Today, and US therapist Facebook communities.

### 7. The business has a process-as-proof problem that the website must solve
With no client portfolio beyond one site, every marketing channel has to work harder. The solution identified in the interactive design section of this brief — using the website itself as the live demonstration of the process (drawing → building in real time as the visitor scrolls) — is not optional. It is the entire credibility mechanism until client work accumulates. Delaying the launch of the site delays every other revenue channel that depends on a portfolio link.

## Part 5: Demand Forces
### Forces Increasing Demand (2026–2030)
The anti-AI design movement is generating genuine commercial demand for hand-made visual work. Adobe recorded a 30% rise in searches for hand-drawn design elements in 2024, and 2026 design trend reports unanimously name tactile, illustrated, human-made aesthetics as the primary counter-movement to AI homogenisation. The private practice therapy sector continues to grow globally as mental health awareness increases and more practitioners move out of institutional employment into independent practice. Multilingual web design demand is rising as businesses seek to reach international audiences. The vibe-coding side hustle has created a wave of low-quality AI sites that small business owners are already becoming disillusioned with — driving demand for quality correction work.

### Forces Decreasing Demand
AI tools are improving rapidly in design quality — the gap between AI-generated and human-made visual polish is narrowing even if original illustration cannot be replicated. Over 75% of web designers report AI competition has increased in the past year. Economic pressure on small businesses (therapists, florists, independent shops) means website investment is discretionary and may be deferred during downturns. The therapy website niche is being targeted by an increasing number of specialist designers, some of whom offer free or very low-cost starter sites to build market share. If the illustrated website format becomes widely imitated, the differentiator erodes — though the multilingual advantage would persist.

## Part 6: Seasonality & Cycle Checklist
### Calendar Cycles
- **January** ↑ demand — New year business planning; therapists setting up private practice; small businesses reassessing online presence
- **September** ↑ demand — Post-summer reset; therapists returning to full caseloads; hospitality businesses planning for next year's season
- **Pre-Christmas / November** ↑ demand for retail and hospitality clients — florists, bookshops, glamping sites want to look their best before peak season
- **June–August** ↓ demand — Therapists often reduce hours; small business owners operationally busy; lower project bandwidth across the board
### Economic Cycles
- **Tax year end (April, UK)** ↑ demand — Business clients with remaining budget may invest before year close
- **Post-recession recovery** ↑ demand — Business relaunch and repositioning; website refresh is part of that
- **Budget squeeze / economic downturn** ↓ demand — Website investment is first-to-cut for small businesses under margin pressure
- **US dollar strength vs GBP** ↑ demand for US clients — favourable exchange rate makes UK designer pricing attractive to American buyers
### Behavioral Cycles
- **Annual private practice re-registration (UK therapists, September–October)** ↑ demand — Therapists renewing BACP/BPS membership often reassess their professional profile
- **Post-social-media disillusionment** ↑ demand — Business owners burned by Instagram algorithm changes reinvesting in owned web presence
- **New business launches (year-round, peak January/September)** ↑ demand — New independent practices and shops always need a first site
- **Therapist training cohort graduations (June, December)** ↑ demand — New therapists entering private practice need their first website

## Part 9: Pricing Experiments
### Experiment 1: Anchor High, Offer One Intro Slot
**Test:** Display a standard rate of £595 with an explicit introductory offer of £395 for the next one or two clients. Frame it as: "While I'm building my portfolio of illustrated sites, I'm offering my first two clients a reduced rate."
**Hypothesis:** Anchoring to £595 makes £395 feel like a deal rather than the standard price. It also explains the discount without devaluing the work.
**Metric:** Number of enquiries; whether clients accept without negotiating further downward. **Risk Level:** Low

### Experiment 2: Productise "The First Page"
**Test:** Name and scope a specific product: one illustrated landing page, one scroll interaction, contact form, delivered in three weeks. Price at £450 / $575 USD.
**Hypothesis:** A named, scoped, time-bounded product reduces buyer hesitation by removing ambiguity about what they receive and when.
**Metric:** Time from first contact to paid deposit; number of back-and-forth exchanges. **Risk Level:** Low

### Experiment 3: Therapy-Specific Package
**Test:** "The Practice Page" — illustrated landing page, therapy-appropriate copy guidance, GDPR-compliant contact form, mobile-first, delivered in two weeks. £495 / $625 USD.
**Hypothesis:** A therapy-specific named product converts faster because therapists feel it was made for them.
**Metric:** Conversion rate from enquiry to paid client; whether therapy-niche posts outperform general posts. **Risk Level:** Low

### Experiment 4: USD Pricing for US Audience
**Test:** Separate Instagram bio link or landing page with pricing in USD ($595 intro / $795 standard), run in parallel with UK GBP pricing.
**Hypothesis:** US buyers converting from USD eliminates hesitation about foreign currency and increases revenue per project at current rates.
**Metric:** Proportion of enquiries from US vs UK; whether USD-priced enquiries convert differently. **Risk Level:** Low

### Experiment 5: Multilingual Outreach as Paid-Service Test
**Test:** Post one piece of content in French ("Je construis des sites web pour les psychologues en cabinet libéral — dessinés à la main, pas générés par IA") with the psychologist site as visual.
**Hypothesis:** French-speaking therapist market has almost no illustrated specialist competition — even one post generates disproportionate interest.
**Metric:** Engagement vs English equivalent; number of French-language enquiries within 2 weeks. **Risk Level:** Very low

## Part 10: Founder Blind Spots
**1. Underpricing as a confidence substitute** — Charging £100 for original illustration plus build time is avoidance of rejection, not modesty. *Safeguard:* set a minimum price before any client conversation (hours × reasonable rate); write it down; do not negotiate below it.

**2. Waiting for the portfolio to feel "ready"** — It never will. The first two clients sign on an imperfect portfolio; it improves fastest once clients pay. *Safeguard:* set a hard public launch date with one real case study and one concept site.

**3. Confirmation bias in the therapy niche** — "I love this" is not "I will pay for this." *Safeguard:* count only paid transactions as product-market fit.

**4. Neglecting the multilingual advantage** — The instinct is to focus on the crowded English UK market. *Safeguard:* ≥1 French and ≥1 Spanish post per week for four weeks; measure before concluding.

**5. Scope creep on early projects** — At intro prices the temptation to add "just one more thing" destroys economics. *Safeguard:* one-paragraph written scope statement; define three revisions; charge for anything outside it.

**6. The pipeline gap** — A fully booked one-person studio stops marketing, then the pipeline is empty. *Safeguard:* one outreach or content action every week, regardless of workload.

**7. Treating distinctiveness as a creative rather than commercial argument** — Distinctive = chosen over competitors = more customers = more money. *Safeguard:* before posting, ask whether it states the commercial gain or only shows something beautiful. Beautiful alone does not sell.
